First-Time Buyer in Ottawa:
Your Complete 2024 Guide

Pre-approval, neighbourhoods, offers, closing costs, and what it actually feels like to buy your first home in Ottawa, all in one place.

Akshay Chauhan, Accliv Group
June 2024  ·  8 min read

Buying your first home in Ottawa is one of the biggest financial decisions you will make. It is also one of the most navigable, once you understand what the process actually looks like and what the Ottawa-specific rules of the road are. This guide covers everything, in the order it matters.

Start With Your Numbers, Not Listings

The most common first-time buyer mistake in Ottawa is starting with listings before understanding finances. You find a house you love, then discover you cannot qualify for the mortgage it requires, or that closing costs you did not budget for are going to strain your savings. The excitement of browsing MLS is real, but do it after you have a pre-approval in hand.

In Canada, the minimum down payment for a home purchase is 5% for properties under $500,000. For the portion of a purchase price between $500,000 and $999,999, a 10% down payment applies. Homes over $1,000,000 require a minimum 20% and are not eligible for CMHC mortgage insurance. Ottawa's average home price in 2024 sits around $650,000–$680,000 for all property types, so most first-time buyers are looking at a blended 5–10% down payment requirement.

Ottawa First-Time Buyer Advantage

Ottawa has no municipal land transfer tax. Unlike Toronto buyers who pay a double land transfer tax (provincial + municipal), Ottawa buyers pay only the Ontario provincial LTT. On a $650,000 first home, eligible first-time buyers pay approximately $4,475 after the Ontario first-time buyer rebate of up to $4,000, versus $20,000+ in Toronto.

Getting Pre-Approved: What It Actually Involves

A mortgage pre-approval is not the same as pre-qualification. A pre-qualification is a quick estimate based on self-reported information. A pre-approval involves submitting your financial documents, T4s, pay stubs, bank statements, Notice of Assessments, and having a lender perform a full credit check. The result is a letter stating the maximum amount they will lend you, at what rate, for what term.

This letter is your license to seriously search. Without it, you cannot submit a competitive offer in Ottawa's active market, and you risk losing a property you want while scrambling to arrange financing after the fact.

  • Contact a mortgage broker or your bank, mortgage brokers access multiple lenders simultaneously
  • Gather: two years of tax returns (NOAs), two recent pay stubs, 3 months of bank statements, proof of down payment
  • Pre-approval rates are typically held for 90–120 days
  • Getting pre-approved does not commit you to that lender, you can shop around at closing

Choosing Your Ottawa Neighbourhood

Ottawa's neighbourhoods are genuinely distinct from one another in price, lifestyle, commute, and long-term value. Choosing the right area for your situation is as important as finding the right property, because the same $650,000 buys radically different things depending on where in the city you are looking.

For first-time buyers, Ottawa's most common entry points are Barrhaven (newer homes, great schools, family community, strong value), Orleans (bilingual community, competitive prices, east Ottawa), and Nepean (established streets, more urban, investor-grade opportunities in older bungalows). Westboro and the Glebe are aspirational for most first-timers given price points, but offer excellent long-term value if your budget stretches.

  • Barrhaven: $550K–$850K detached, family-oriented, suburban but complete
  • Orleans: $500K–$750K, bilingual community, highway access, good schools
  • Nepean: $550K–$850K, central, diverse housing stock, value play
  • Kanata: $600K–$1M, tech hub, strong rental demand for investors

What the Buying Process Actually Looks Like

Once your pre-approval is in place and you have a clear sense of your target area, the typical Ottawa first-time purchase moves through these stages: active search (2–8 weeks of showings), offer (1–3 attempts in a typical market), conditional period (7–14 days for inspection and financing), and closing (30–90 days from offer acceptance).

Your buyer's agent prepares the Agreement of Purchase and Sale, a legally binding offer document. Standard conditions for first-time Ottawa buyers include a financing condition (giving you time to formally arrange your mortgage) and a home inspection condition (allowing a professional to assess the property before you commit).

In Ottawa's spring market (March–May), competition increases and conditions can be harder to include. In the fall and winter, the market calms and conditions are more standard. Your agent should guide your strategy based on current conditions, not generic advice.

Closing Costs: The Number Nobody Talks About Enough

First-time buyers often budget for the down payment but underestimate closing costs. On a $650,000 Ottawa purchase, expect to budget an additional $8,000–$20,000 depending on your situation. This covers Ontario land transfer tax (net of your first-time buyer rebate), legal fees, title insurance, home inspection, and property tax adjustments.

The single largest variable is whether you qualify for Ontario's first-time buyer land transfer tax rebate (up to $4,000) and whether the Home Buyers' Plan allows you to use RRSP funds for your down payment (up to $35,000 per person, $70,000 per couple under the 2024 limits).

The First-Time Buyer's Most Common Mistakes

After seeing dozens of first-time buyer situations, the patterns are consistent. These are the mistakes that cost people money or deals in Ottawa's market:

  • Starting the search before getting pre-approved, you will fall in love with something you cannot buy
  • Confusing asking price with market value, sellers set asking prices, buyers and market data determine actual value
  • Skipping the home inspection to be competitive, understand the risk before waiving conditions, not after
  • Buying at the top of their pre-approval limit, your lender's maximum is not your comfort maximum
  • Ignoring closing costs, budget 1.5–4% of purchase price on top of your down payment
  • Making major financial changes during the process, new car, job change, or new credit can void your pre-approval

Working With a Buyer's Agent in Ottawa

In Ontario, buyer representation costs you nothing. The seller pays the total commission, which is split between the listing and buyer's agents. You receive full professional representation, access to Ottawa MLS listings, market analysis, offer preparation, and someone legally obligated to act in your interest throughout the transaction, at no direct cost.
