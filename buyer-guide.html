Ottawa Buyer's Guide

How to Buy a Home
in Ottawa

A clear, honest walkthrough of the Ottawa purchase process — from getting pre-approved to picking up your keys. No jargon, no pressure.

RECO

Your Rights as an Ontario Real Estate Consumer

Under Ontario's Trust in Real Estate Services Act, 2002 (TRESA), your agent must provide you with the RECO Information Guide before providing any services or signing any representation agreement. This guide explains your rights as a client, what to expect from your registrant, and the risks of proceeding as a self-represented party. 01
Step One

Get Your Finances in Order

Before you look at a single listing, you need to know what you can actually afford and obtain a mortgage pre-approval. This is not just a formality — a pre-approval letter tells sellers you are a serious buyer and establishes your upper limit before emotions get involved.

In Canada, the minimum down payment is 5% for homes under $500,000, with the portion between $500,000 and $999,999 requiring 10%. Homes at $1,000,000 or above require a minimum 20% down payment and are not eligible for mortgage insurance. Ottawa's average home price currently sits in the $620,000–$680,000 range, meaning most buyers need at least 5–10% plus closing costs in savings before they start.

  • Contact a mortgage broker or your bank to get pre-approved before searching
  • Pre-approval is not pre-qualification — it involves a full credit check and income verification
  • Know the difference between your pre-approved amount and what you are comfortable paying monthly
  • Factor in closing costs: budget an additional 1.5–4% of purchase price on top of your down payment
Ottawa Tip

Ottawa has no municipal land transfer tax. Unlike Toronto buyers who pay a double land transfer tax (provincial + municipal), Ottawa buyers only pay the Ontario provincial LTT. On a $650,000 home, this saves you roughly $4,475 compared to a Toronto purchase.

02
Step Two

Define Your Search

Ottawa is a city of distinct neighbourhoods, each with its own price dynamics, lifestyle, and buyer profile. Knowing the difference between Westboro and Barrhaven is not just geography — it is the difference between a good decision and a great one.

Start by separating your wants from your needs. A second bathroom might be negotiable. A school district might not. A third bedroom for a home office might matter more than a finished basement. Write these down before you start viewing properties — it is easy to get distracted by a beautifully staged home that does not actually fit your life.

  • Which areas make sense for your commute, lifestyle, and budget?
  • Detached, semi-detached, townhome, or condo? Each has different resale dynamics in Ottawa
  • What is your five-year plan — are you buying for lifestyle, investment, or both?
  • New build or resale? New builds in Barrhaven, Kanata, and Stittsville have builder risk; resale is more established

03
Step Three

Work with a Buyer's Agent

In Ontario, buyer representation costs you nothing. The seller pays the total commission — typically 3.5–5% of the sale price — which is then split between the listing agent and the buyer's agent. You get full professional representation, market access, negotiation expertise, and contract guidance at no direct cost to you.

Your buyer's agent is legally obligated to act in your interest, not the seller's. That means honest pricing advice even when it is not what you want to hear, strategic offer structuring, and a representative who has handled dozens of transactions when you are handling your first or second.

  • In Ontario, buyer's agents must be licensed under RECO (Real Estate Council of Ontario)
  • Members of OREB (Ottawa Real Estate Board) have access to the Ottawa MLS — the complete listing database
  • A buyer representation agreement formalizes the relationship — it defines the agent's obligations to you
  • Interview more than one agent before committing — you will work closely together for 1–3 months
04
Step Four

Make the Right Offer

An offer in Ontario is a legally binding document called an Agreement of Purchase and Sale. Your agent will prepare it based on the negotiated price, closing date, and any conditions you wish to include. The seller can accept, reject, or counter — and the process can move quickly, especially in a competitive Ottawa spring market.

Standard conditions in an Ottawa offer include: financing condition (giving you time to finalize your mortgage), home inspection condition (right to a professional inspection within a set number of days), and for condos, a status certificate condition (reviewing the building's financial and legal health).

  • In a buyer's market, conditional offers are standard and expected
  • In a seller's market (Ottawa's typical spring: March–May), fewer conditions may be required to compete
  • The deposit is typically 1–5% of the purchase price, due within 24 hours of acceptance
  • Closing date is negotiable — often 30–90 days from offer acceptance
Ottawa Market Pattern

Ottawa's real estate market historically heats up in late February and peaks in April–May. If you're buying in this window, expect competition. Fall (September–November) tends to be calmer, with more room for conditions and negotiation.

05
Step Five

Inspection & Due Diligence

A home inspection is a professional assessment of the property's physical condition — roof, foundation, plumbing, electrical, HVAC, windows, insulation. It does not guarantee the house has no problems; it reveals what is visible and accessible on the day of inspection. Budget $400–$600 for a standard Ottawa home inspection.

During your inspection period, your lawyer will review title, check for liens or encumbrances, and confirm the property can transfer cleanly. For condos, your lawyer will review the status certificate — a package of financial statements, bylaws, and meeting minutes that reveals the health of the building's reserve fund and any special assessments.

  • Never waive an inspection on a property you have not independently verified
  • Choose an inspector who is a member of CAHPI (Canadian Association of Home and Property Inspectors)
  • Attend the inspection in person — what the inspector says on site is as valuable as the written report
  • Use inspection findings as information, not always as a renegotiation tool
06
Step Six

Close & Take Possession

On closing day, your lawyer finalizes all the paperwork and transfers funds from your mortgage lender and savings to the seller. You do not need to be present — everything is handled through your real estate lawyer's trust account. Once the land transfer is registered at the Ontario Land Registry, your agent picks up the keys and you officially own the property.

In the days before closing, your lender will complete a final review (mortgage advances only when conditions are met), your lawyer will prepare a statement of adjustments (prorated property taxes, utility adjustments, etc.), and you will do a final walkthrough to confirm the property is in the agreed condition.

  • Ensure home insurance is in place before your closing date — your lender requires it
  • Closing costs are due on closing day via certified cheque or wire to your lawyer's trust account
  • Book your move for the afternoon — registration at the land registry typically completes by midday
  • Change the locks on your first day. It is standard practice regardless of the seller.

The Ottawa Advantage: No Municipal Land Transfer Tax

Toronto buyers pay two land transfer taxes — provincial and municipal — effectively doubling the cost. Ottawa buyers pay only the Ontario provincial LTT. On the same $650,000 purchase, a Toronto buyer would pay approximately $20,950 in combined LTT versus $8,475 in Ottawa. That $12,475 difference stays in your pocket.

Common Questions

What Buyers Ask Most

Do I need a real estate agent to buy a home in Ottawa?

Technically no — but buyer representation costs you nothing (the seller pays the commission), and gives you access to MLS, professional negotiation, legal form preparation, and someone whose job it is to act in your interest. Buying without representation is legal but rarely advantageous for a buyer.

How long does it take to buy a home in Ottawa?

From starting your search to getting keys, plan for 60–90 days in a typical market. This includes 2–8 weeks of searching, 1–3 weeks to firm up an accepted offer, and 30–90 days of closing period. In a competitive spring market, the search period can compress significantly if you are decisive.

What is the Ottawa land transfer tax and how much will I pay?

Ontario has a provincial land transfer tax calculated in tiers: 0.5% on the first $55,000, 1.0% on $55,001–$250,000, 1.5% on $250,001–$400,000, and 2.0% on $400,001–$2,000,000. On a $650,000 purchase this totals approximately $8,475. First-time buyers in Ontario qualify for a rebate of up to $4,000, reducing the net amount to around $4,475. Ottawa has no additional municipal LTT.

Should I get a home inspection if it might cost me the deal?

In a very competitive market, some buyers waive conditions to win. This is a personal risk decision — not a recommendation we make lightly. If you do proceed without an inspection condition, you should at minimum get a pre-offer inspection (before submitting) or purchase a home warranty. Never waive an inspection because you feel pressure. Only do so after understanding the risks.

What is the best time of year to buy in Ottawa?

Ottawa's spring market (February–May) sees the highest listings volume but also the most competition. Fall (September–November) typically offers more choice and less competing buyer pressure, with motivated sellers who listed in spring without selling. Winter is the quietest — low inventory but the buyers who are active are serious, which can work in your favour.
